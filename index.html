Rethinking Number Theory

L-functions: What, why, how?

Let $E$ be an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$.

Example: The curve 11.a2 has equation $y^2=x^3-13392x-1080432$

Often, we want to study arithmetic of $E$, in particular gain information about $E(\mathbb{Q}$.

The L-function associated to $E$ is the Euler product$\small ^\ast$
\[ \small L(E,s) = \prod_p \frac{1}{1 - a_p p^{-s} + p^{1-2s}} = \sum_{n \ge 1} \frac{a_n}{n^s}\]

  • $a_p$ encode info about number of points of $E$ mod $p$
  • has analytic continuation to $\mathbb{C}$
  • satisfies functional equation relating $\small L(E,s)$ and $\small L(E,2-s)$

$^\ast$ ignoring primes $p$ of bad reduction for simplicity

Given $E/\mathbb{Q}$ and a Dirichlet character $\chi \colon \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{C}$, we can form the twisted $L$-function $\enskip L(E,s,\chi) = \sum_{n \ge 1} \chi(n) a_n n^{-s} $

By BSD, the order of vanishing of $L(E,s,\chi)$ at $s=1$ should be related to the rank of $E(K)$ over the extension $K/\mathbb{Q}$ associated to $\chi$.

Motto: Interesting arithmetic data is encoded in the location of the zeroes (and poles) of L-functions; since L-functions are analytic objects, one can use analytic tools to study arithmetic.

Dirichlet Characters

A Dirichlet character is a function $\chi \colon \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{C}$ together with a positive integer $d$ called the modulus, such that for all integers $m$ and $n$

  • $\chi(mn) = \chi(m) \chi(n)$
  • $\chi(n + d) = \chi(n)$

The conductor of a Dirichlet character $\chi$ of modulus $d$ is the smallest positive integer $q$ dividing $d$ such that \[ \chi(m) = \chi(n)\] whenever $m \equiv n \bmod{q}\,$ and $\, \gcd(mn,d) = 1$.

The character $\chi$ is said to be primitive if the conductor $q$ is equal to the modulus $d$
